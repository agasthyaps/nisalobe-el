Anchor Chart

(Example for Teacher Reference)

Characteristics of Narrative Nonfiction

- Conveys factual information in a story format

- Contains extensive research and many factual details are incorporated into the story

- May include a bibliography of research sources

- Utilizes the techniques of literary storytelling, such as dialogue and **character** development

- Has dialogue that may not be exact but rather representative of what historical figures might have said based on researched information

- Portrays the historical figure as a **character** or the historical event as the **conflict** or **setting**

- Has detailed illustrations or photographs that accurately portray the real people and events

- May contain text features common in nonfiction texts, such as timelines, biographies, or glossaries

- Often includes an author’s note or a foreword about the true aspects of the story and/or how the author became inspired to write the book

- Often has title that includes a subtitle using words like **how** or **the true story of** to clarify that the book is nonfiction

- Often features the subject of the book on the cover

- Attempts to both entertain and inform the reader; often celebrates an important achievement in history

- Considers the audience and matches style and tone to appeal to that age group

- Can be written in different formats, such as memoir, biographies, autobiographies, travel writing, etc.

Note: A text does not <u>have</u> to fulfill ALL of these criteria to be considered narrative nonfiction.
