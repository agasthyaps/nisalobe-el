<td>W.8.3a</td>
<td>I organize events in a sequence that unfolds naturally and logically.</td>
<td><strong>A significant event from the upstander’s life unfolds naturally and logically through the answers to the questions posed in the interview.</strong></td>
<td></td>
</tr>

<td>W.8.3c, W.8.4</td>
<td>I use a variety of transitional words and phrases to sequence events and to show changes in time or in setting.</td>
<td><strong>Transitional words and phrases in the narrative sequence the events in the story, “speed up” time, and ensure that the interview flows seamlessly from one event to the next in a clear and organized fashion (e.g., “at first” and “after”).</strong></td>
<td></td>
</tr>
<tr class="odd">
<td>W.8.3c, W.8.4</td>
<td>I use transitional words and phrases to show the relationships among experiences and events.</td>
<td><strong>Transitional words and phrases connect events in the upstander’s experience to create an arc that has a beginning, middle, and end (e.g., “I spent,” “I began,” and “then,”).</strong></td>
<td></td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td>W.8.3b</td>
<td>I develop experiences, events, and characters using techniques such as dialogue, pacing, description, and reflection.</td>
<td><p><strong>There are places in the narrative interview that speed up and slow down pacing to engage the reader in the most relevant events of the plot (e.g., “I watched the soldiers muscle him into a military truck and drive away,” and “the baker’s apron hanging there on a hook as if the baker had just stepped out for a moment,”).</strong></p>
<p><strong>Reflection is used to show what the interviewee feels and thinks about their experiences.</strong></p></td>
<td></td>

<td><strong>Descriptive details, sensory language, and precisely chosen words capture a significant moment from the upstander’s life. (e.g., “As the siren’s scream filled the air” and “the muffled screams of the baker”).</strong></td>
